## Persona 1: Dev-First Builder

**Name:** Marco, 29
**Role:** Full-stack developer at a Series A startup
**Location:** Berlin, Germany
**Experience:** 5 years shipping features, 0 years thinking about brand

### Demographics
- Uses Claude Code daily, previously Cursor
- Ships 3-4x faster with AI than a year ago
- Runs a side project (SaaS tool) that desperately needs a landing page
- Reads Hacker News, follows AI coding Twitter, lurks in Discord communities

### Goals
- Ship a side project that looks like a real product, not a hackathon demo
- Stop embarrassing himself with landing pages that scream "developer made this"
- Learn enough design thinking to make confident visual decisions without hiring a designer
- Build a brand that holds up as the product grows

### Pain Points
- Every time he asks Claude to "make this look nice," he gets different results. No consistency across pages.
- He can spot good design (admires Linear, Raycast, Vercel) but cannot articulate or reproduce why they look good.
- Figma feels like learning a second profession. He wants design decisions in the terminal where he already works.
- Component libraries (shadcn/ui) give him buttons and cards, but not a coherent visual identity.

### Brand Expectations
- CLI-native. If it requires opening a browser or a GUI, he will not use it.
- Opinionated. Tell him what to do, do not give him 50 options. He wants a mentor, not a menu.
- Fast. Must integrate into his existing AI workflow, not add a separate process.
- Cool. The tool itself must look and feel like something he is proud to use. Terminal aesthetic, not corporate.

### Device Usage
- MacBook Pro (primary), iPhone (secondary)
- Terminal: iTerm2 / Warp / Ghostty with a dark theme
- Browser: Arc or Chrome, dark mode always
- Design tools: literally none (the problem)

### Media Habits
- Hacker News daily, Twitter/X for AI coding discourse
- YouTube for technical content (Fireship, Theo)
- Reads changelogs of tools he admires (Linear, Vercel)
- Does not follow design blogs — GSP must meet him where he is

## Persona 2: Terminal-Curious Designer

**Name:** Suki, 33
**Role:** Senior product designer at a mid-size tech company
**Location:** San Francisco, CA
**Experience:** 8 years in product design, comfortable in Figma, curious about AI tools

### Demographics
- Expert in Figma, uses it daily
- Has watched vibe coding demos and feels both excited and threatened
- Tried Claude Code once, found it powerful but disorienting — no visual feedback
- Reads Design Twitter, follows design system teams at Linear, Stripe, Vercel

### Goals
- Stay relevant as AI changes how products are built
- Bridge the gap between her design thinking and the terminal where products are increasingly being created
- Stop handing off specs that get lost in translation. She wants to participate in the build.
- Bring craft and coherence to AI-generated output that she sees as "close but soulless"

### Pain Points
- She knows what good design looks like but cannot translate that knowledge into CLI workflows
- Developers on her team use Claude Code to build UIs without consulting her. The results are functional but brand-inconsistent.
- The handoff problem is getting worse, not better. AI makes building so fast that the design review loop gets skipped.
- She feels disconnected from the new era of conversational creation. Her tools (Figma, Storybook) are one step removed from where products are now built.

### Brand Expectations
- Design-literate. The tool must speak her language — hierarchy, whitespace, type scale, color system.
- Not dumbed down. She has real expertise; she needs a bridge, not training wheels.
- Process-oriented. Discovery, strategy, identity, system — this maps to how she already thinks about brand work.
- Craft-forward. The tool itself must demonstrate the design quality it promises to deliver.

### Device Usage
- MacBook Pro (primary), iPad (Figma on the go)
- Terminal: just started using it (Warp, because it feels more designed)
- Browser: Safari or Arc, follows design trends through bookmarked blogs
- Design tools: Figma (expert), Storybook (familiar), FigJam (for workshops)

### Media Habits
- Design Twitter, Dribbble for inspiration, Figma Community
- Reads NN/g, Smashing Magazine, the Figma blog
- Attends Config, local design meetups
- Follows design system teams: Primer (GitHub), Polaris (Shopify), Carbon (IBM)

## Persona 3: The Design Engineer

**Name:** Ren, 27
**Role:** Design engineer at a developer tools company (or freelance)
**Location:** Tokyo / remote
**Experience:** 4 years straddling design and development. Codes in TypeScript, designs in Figma, thinks in systems.

### Demographics
- Equally comfortable in VS Code and Figma
- Uses Claude Code for implementation, Figma for exploration
- Maintains a personal design system for side projects
- Active in the design engineering community (follows Vercel, Raycast, Linear design teams)

### Goals
- Find tools that match the way they already work — across design and code simultaneously
- Build products that are both technically excellent and visually coherent from day one
- Evangelize design engineering as a discipline. Wants the world to recognize this role.
- Ship faster without sacrificing craft. AI should amplify their hybrid skills, not flatten them.

### Pain Points
- Existing tools force a choice: design tool OR development tool. Nothing bridges both naturally.
- They manually maintain brand coherence across projects — creating tokens, documenting color systems, writing style guides by hand.
- Vibe-coded products from non-designer developers look mediocre. They want to raise the floor, not just the ceiling.
- The design engineering role is still poorly understood. "Are you a designer or a developer?" is a question they are tired of answering.

### Brand Expectations
- Hybrid-native. Must respect both design and development disciplines.
- Systematic. They think in tokens, scales, and systems. The tool must output at this level of specificity.
- Extensible. They will want to customize, contribute, and extend. Open source is non-negotiable.
- Identity-affirming. The tool should validate the design engineering discipline. It should feel like "this was made for me."

### Device Usage
- MacBook Pro (primary), external monitor for dual Figma+terminal
- Terminal: Ghostty or Kitty (performance-focused, minimal)
- Browser: Arc, with organized spaces for design research and development
- Design tools: Figma (advanced), VS Code (daily), Storybook (for documentation)

### Media Habits
- Design engineering Twitter/Bluesky, Raycast blog, Linear changelog
- Follows individual design engineers (Rauno Freiberg, Paco Coursey, Emil Kowalski)
- Reads CSS-Tricks, Josh Comeau, Adam Argyle
- Contributes to open source, attends both design and dev conferences

## Audience Convergence

All three personas converge on a shared identity: **builders** — people who craft products, not just features. The developer needs design thinking. The designer needs terminal access. The design engineer needs a tool that validates their hybrid identity.

GSP's brand must speak to all three without alienating any. The key: **lead with the builder identity, not the job title.**
