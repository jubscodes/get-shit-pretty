## Supporting Messages

### Message 1: For Developers (Marco)

**"You shipped the code. Now ship the brand."**

Developers build things that work. GSP helps them build things that hold together -- not just functionally, but as a coherent product with positioning, voice, and visual identity. The terminal is already home. The pipeline is already familiar (phases, gates, outputs). GSP adds the design layer without asking developers to leave their environment.

**Proof points:**
- Structured pipeline mirrors dev workflows: phases, outputs, iteration, version control
- Terminal-native -- no context switch to Figma or a browser
- Generates design tokens that map directly to code (CSS custom properties, Tailwind config, component props)
- The brief phase turns "make it look nice" into a real brand strategy -- the developer doesn't need design training, just the willingness to answer honest questions
- Multi-runtime: works with Claude Code, OpenCode, Gemini CLI, Codex -- wherever the developer already works

### Message 2: For Designers (Suki)

**"Stop handing off. Start building."**

Designers are losing relevance as AI generates UI from prompts. GSP gives designers a bridge to the terminal -- a tool that speaks their language (brand strategy, visual systems, design tokens) but lives in the environment where products actually ship. Design engineering is the new discipline. GSP is how designers get there.

**Proof points:**
- Uses real design frameworks: Kapferer's brand prism, Jungian archetypes, positioning maps, messaging hierarchies -- not dumbed-down "pick a color" workflows
- Outputs are design-system-native: tokens, component specs, Storybook stories, accessibility checks
- The pipeline validates design decisions against strategy -- no more "the developer changed my colors"
- Open source and ecosystem-connected: works with Figma, not against it
- Designers who use GSP don't hand off a PDF -- they ship a system

### Message 3: For Builders / Design Engineers (Ren)

**"Finally, a tool that validates the hybrid."**

Design engineering is real, but the tooling doesn't reflect it. Design tools assume you don't code. Dev tools assume you don't design. GSP is built for the intersection -- the builder who thinks in systems, writes in code, and cares about brand coherence. The dual-diamond pipeline is for you. The specialized agents speak both languages. The output is both strategic and shippable.

**Proof points:**
- Dual-diamond pipeline: branding diamond (discover, strategy, verbal, identity, system) + project diamond (brief, research, design, critique, build, review)
- Agents with domain expertise: brand strategist, verbal identity writer, visual designer, design engineer
- Chunked output: modular, composable, versionable -- the way real design systems work
- Category creator: GSP defines "design engineering system" as a tool category. It didn't exist before.
- Community-driven: open source, forkable, extensible -- built by builders, for builders

## Messaging Principles

1. **Show, don't claim.** Never say "powerful" or "innovative." Show the pipeline. Show the output. Show the before/after. Let the work prove the claim.

2. **Precise language.** "Design engineering system" not "AI-powered design platform." "Structured journey" not "seamless experience." "Brand that lasts" not "beautiful designs." Every word is chosen.

3. **Irreverent where appropriate.** The name is "Get Shit Pretty." The tagline has a swear word. The tone can be direct, funny, blunt. But irreverence serves clarity -- it's never random, never mean, never at the user's expense.

4. **Respect the audience's intelligence.** Developers know what design tokens are. Designers know what brand prisms are. Design engineers know both. Don't explain concepts the audience already understands. Do explain why GSP's approach to those concepts is different.

5. **The constraint is the story.** "In your terminal" is not a limitation to explain away. It's the headline. The medium is the message. CLI-native is the feature, not the compromise.

## Ecosystem Narrative

### How to talk about the three brands together

GSP doesn't exist in isolation. It's one of three brands by jubscodes, and the ecosystem itself is a proof point for what GSP does.

**The origin story:**
"jubscodes built GSP to solve a real problem -- their own. jubs.studio, the founder's personal brand, was designed using GSP v0.1 and v0.2. The tool designed the designer. That's not a case study; it's the reason GSP exists."

**The ecosystem pitch:**
"Three brands, one maker. jubs.studio is the founder -- cyberpunk, opinionated, neon green and burnt orange. cyphercn is the component library -- terminal-native blocks, reliable and quiet, carries the green. GSP is the design journey -- structured, unexpected, carries the burnt orange. Same dark-first DNA. Same monospace craft. Three distinct identities that prove what brand coherence actually looks like."

**When to reference the ecosystem:**
- On the GSP landing page: "Built by jubscodes. jubs.studio was designed with GSP."
- In talks/presentations: the ecosystem diagram shows three distinct brands from one maker
- In documentation: cyphercn as an integration example, not a requirement
- Never: as a "suite" or "platform" or "product family"

**The proof point in action:**
"jubs.studio was built with GSP" is the single most important proof point in the messaging arsenal. It answers the skeptic's question -- "does this actually work?" -- with evidence, not claims. Use it:
- In the positioning statement's reason to believe
- In the elevator pitch when space allows
- On the landing page as a visual case study (before/after or process breakdown)
- In the README as social proof
